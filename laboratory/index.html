Laboratory in Adelaide

Site investigations you can build on.

LEARN MORE

In Adelaide, professional laboratory testing forms the backbone of reliable geotechnical assessment, translating field samples into verifiable engineering parameters. Our facility addresses the unique geological profile of the Adelaide Plains and foothills, where reactive clay soils, calcareous sands, and variable alluvial deposits demand precise classification. Adhering to AS 1289 methods, we quantify the behaviour of these materials to support safe, compliant design. The transition from field observation to quantitative data begins with a thorough investigation, ensuring samples are representative of the site stratigraphy before any test procedure commences.

Our testing methodology strictly follows Australian Standards, employing a rigorous quality management system calibrated for local lithologies. Classification tests, including grain size analysis by sieve and hydrometer, define the particle size distribution fundamental to drainage and frost heave assessments. For fine-grained soils prevalent in areas like Keswick or Prospect, Atterberg limits testing precisely determines the plastic and liquid limits, delivering the plasticity index critical for footing designs on expansive clays. These index properties are correlated with advanced strength and consolidation data, ensuring every result meets the traceability required by AS 1289.

Projects across metropolitan Adelaide and regional South Australia benefit from this data, ranging from residential slabs on class M/H sites to infrastructure corridors in the Barossa. When in-situ verification is required, we pair lab-derived moisture-density relationships with field testing, notably the field density test (sand cone method), to validate compaction levels in structural fill. This integrated approach is essential for transport yards, stormwater basins, and commercial foundations, where a discrepancy between laboratory optimum moisture content and field placement can lead to long-term settlement or pavement failure in our Mediterranean climate.

Our streamlined process manages samples from extraction at the drill rig to final certification. Clients receive comprehensive, compliant reports that present clear particle size distribution curves, Atterberg limit values, and compaction characteristics, removing ambiguity from the design phase. By combining local geological insight with rigorous AS 1289 testing, we provide engineers and builders in Adelaide a direct path to defensible bearing capacities and earthwork specifications, effectively de-risking construction on the region’s challenging reactive soils.

Q&A

What is the typical cost range for anchor design on an Adelaide project?

The engineering design fee for a ground anchor system typically falls between AU$1.840 and AU$5.940, depending on the complexity of the wall, the number of anchor rows, and the required testing regime. This covers the geotechnical analysis, anchor configuration drawings, and construction specifications compliant with AS 4678.

What is the difference between active and passive anchors in practice?

An active anchor is tensioned against the structure during installation, applying a pre-compressive force to the retained ground immediately. A passive anchor, or soil nail, is not tensioned; it relies on ground deformation to develop its tensile resistance. In Adelaide, active anchors are standard for soldier pile walls in the CBD, while passive nails suit shallower slope cuts in the foothills.

How do you determine the bond length in Adelaide's rock?

The bond length is calculated by dividing the design working load by the perimeter of the drill hole and the ultimate bond stress of the rock. We derive the bond stress from in-situ pull-out tests on sacrificial anchors, as the weathered profile of the Adelaide Geosyncline rocks varies too much to rely on published correlations alone.

What corrosion protection is required for permanent anchors in Adelaide?

Permanent anchors must have Class II double-corrosion protection as a minimum per AS 4678. This means the tendon is encapsulated in a corrugated plastic sheathing and the entire assembly is grouted inside a second corrugated duct. This is mandatory for the aggressive, often saline, groundwater encountered in the western suburbs.
