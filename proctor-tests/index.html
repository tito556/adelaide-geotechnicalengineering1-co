Proctor Compaction Testing Adelaide: Standard & Modified for Earthworks

Site investigations you can build on.

The contrast between the heavy clays of the Adelaide Plains and the sandy, micaceous soils up in the foothills around Burnside is stark. On the plains, you are often battling reactive ground that swells and shrinks with every season; move east towards the ranges and the decomposed rock creates a completely different fill scenario. Getting the moisture–density relationship right in either profile is what separates a pavement that will last decades from one that rutts after the first winter. In our experience, the Proctor test is the first real check on whether your site won material will behave under a roller. We regularly support earthworks contractors from Port Adelaide to Mount Barker with both standard and modified compactive effort protocols, ensuring the lab curve actually reflects what the plant can achieve on the pad.

A shift of just 2% in moisture content away from optimum can drop field density by over 5% — enough to fail a lift on an Adelaide subdivision.

Method and coverage

Adelaide’s post-war expansion onto Quaternary alluvium and deeply weathered Neoproterozoic bedrock created a patchwork of fill sites that still challenge earthworks supervisors today. Much of the older fill across suburbs like Prospect and Kilburn was placed long before modern compaction control, meaning redevelopment now requires rigorous moisture conditioning and density verification. The Proctor test defines the target dry density ratio (typically 95% or 98% of maximum dry density) that becomes the contractual pass/fail line for each lift. When we run the standard effort (AS 1289.5.1.1 using a 2.7 kg rammer dropped 300 mm) and the modified effort (4.9 kg rammer dropped 450 mm), the difference can be 5–8% in maximum dry density for a clayey sand. We often combine this with a grain size analysis to understand the gradation driving the curve shape, and for road projects we pair it with the CBR test to link compaction to bearing strength under traffic.

Regional considerations

A 4-storey mixed-use project on Greenhill Road sticks in the memory. The contractor had imported a crushed rock fill with a specified modified maximum dry density of 2.18 t/m³, but field densities kept coming in under 92%. The lab curve had been run on a bulk sample that was not representative of the finer, dustier material actually arriving on site. Running a fresh Proctor on the as-received stockpile showed the real maximum dry density was 2.09 t/m³ — the fill was perfectly compacted, but the target was wrong. Using an unverified curve or assuming a generic value for Adelaide limestone rubble will lead to either unnecessary rework or, worse, acceptance of under-compacted fill. The cost of a single re-test is trivial compared to the delay of ripping up and re-rolling several hundred square metres of structural fill.

Q&A

Which Proctor test do I need — standard or modified?

It depends on the project specification. Most residential slabs and landscaping in Adelaide reference the standard Proctor. Commercial building pads, DIT roadworks, and heavy industrial slabs almost always require the modified Proctor. If the spec says '98% modified' you cannot substitute a standard curve. When in doubt, send us the geotechnical spec and we will confirm which method applies before starting the test.

What does a Proctor test cost in Adelaide?

A standard or modified Proctor test in our Adelaide laboratory ranges from AU$160 to AU$330 depending on mould size, whether oversize corrections are needed, and how many points we run on the curve. A single-point field check is at the lower end; a full 5-point curve with particle density determination sits at the upper end. We provide a fixed quote before any work begins.

How much sample do you need for a Proctor test?

For a standard 105 mm mould we need about 20 kg of representative material. For the 152 mm mould (used when coarser particles are present) we need roughly 35–40 kg. The sample should be bagged and sealed immediately after excavation to preserve its natural moisture. If the material is particularly dry and dusty, wet it slightly before bagging to avoid fines loss during transport to our Adelaide lab.
